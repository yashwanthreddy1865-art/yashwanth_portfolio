Automobile Suspension Redesign & Optimization (FEA)

Key Technical Skills:

Finite Element Analysis (FEA), Structural Optimization, Modal (Vibration) Analysis, Non-linear Stress Simulation, Material Science (Titanium/Aluminum Alloys), ANSYS Workbench, CAD Geometry.

Functionality/Methodology:

Performed a comprehensive FEA on an automobile suspension assembly (lower arm and strut) to optimize for weight reduction while maintaining safety standards. Applied rigorous boundary conditions and simulated two extreme static load cases, up to 28,000 N. The methodology involved three material and geometry iterations to balance performance, cost, and manufacturability, ultimately recommending the most cost-efficient and lightweight design.

Quantifiable Results:

  • Weight Reduction: Achieved a total assembly weight of 27.58 kg (the lightest design).
  • Structural Integrity: Maintained a minimum Factor of Safety (FOS) of 1.43 under all load conditions.
  • Stress Control: Maximum von-Mises stress (204 MPa) safely below Aluminum alloy's yield strength (280 MPa).
  • Dynamic Performance: First Natural Frequency of 86.62 Hz, exceeding the target of 80 Hz.

PCB Steady-State Heat Transfer Optimization

Key Technical Skills:

Applied Conduction Heat Transfer, Computational Thermal Analysis, Electronics Cooling, Design Optimization, Cost/Weight Trade-off Analysis, Simulation Modeling (Processors, Heat Sinks), ANSYS.

Functionality/Methodology:

Conducted a thermal analysis to optimize a Printed Circuit Board (PCB) cooling system under steady-state heat transfer. The core methodology involved modeling heat dissipation from multiple components (Processors, Chips) and evaluating three design iterations based on varying thermal conductance materials and fan convection coefficients (h). The final selection was driven by a weighted matrix balancing temperature control, cost, and total weight.

Quantifiable Results:

  • Cost-Effective Solution: Identified and recommended the most cost-effective design (Iteration 1) with a total cost of $65 and balanced weight of 0.5154 kg.
  • Trade-off Analysis: Lightest alternative (0.4721 kg) would incur significantly higher cost ($125).
  • Thermal Margin: Selected configuration successfully kept maximum component temperature below critical threshold (70°C).

Mesh Quality & Element Type Evaluation (FEA Validation)

Key Technical Skills:

Finite Element Methods (FEM) Theory, Simulation Validation, Stress Concentration Analysis, Meshing Strategy/Refinement, Error Analysis, ANSYS Workbench.

Functionality/Methodology:

A validation study to quantify the influence of meshing parameters (density and element type) on the accuracy of FEA results, focusing on a component with a known theoretical solution (45,250 psi). The methodology involved systematically testing six configurations: 4-Node Linear vs. 8-Node Quadratic elements, each across Coarse, Medium, and Fine mesh densities to establish best practices for future simulations.

Quantifiable Results:

  • Maximum Accuracy: 8-Node Quadratic Element with Fine Mesh achieved near-perfect correlation with theory, simulating 45,312 psi.
  • Best Practice Recommendation: Established 8-Node Quadratic Element as superior for accurate, smooth stress prediction in high-concentration regions.
  • Efficiency Recommendation: Medium Mesh with 8-Node elements offers optimal balance between computational efficiency and result accuracy.
